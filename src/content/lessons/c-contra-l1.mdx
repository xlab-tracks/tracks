It might initially feel quite obvious as to why AI control would probably be good. After all, acquiring misalignment evidence, ensuring useful work of misaligned models, and having additive safety measures that generally don't interfere with alignment work, seems great.

To begin this module we will

- Illustrate each argument against control
- Evaluate and attempt to remedy each argument
- See how useful control actually is

Many of the seminal "arguments against control" lay in the following post — [The Case Against AI Control Research](https://www.lesswrong.com/posts/8wBN8cdNAv3c7vt6p/the-case-against-ai-control-research). Your time would be well spent reading through it. Here we will address the two main arguments this piece makes.

## Control's Main Mechanism for Reducing X-risk

Control's mechanism for reduction in X-risk is centered around the claims that

- We will use transformatively useful AIs ([TUAIs](https://arc.net/l/quote/lypsxezs)) to align and build the following generation of AIs.
- These AIs will likely be misaligned, or we may be unable to determine their alignment with strong certainty as claimed in module 1. At worst they are actively scheming while we are unaware.
- Should we pursue automated [AI R&D](https://arc.net/l/quote/agmutdew) / [RSI](https://arc.net/l/quote/clgjqvqb) in this regime, it would be great to have a catch-all measure where even if AIs are scheming, they cannot act on their motivations. Or, any attempted actions are unable to produce negative outcomes.
- Given this catch-all, we would be able to prevent any scheming-induced catastrophic behaviors such as [exfiltrations](https://arc.net/l/quote/qmhxcfvl), [rogue deployments](https://arc.net/l/quote/crogmequ), [research sabotages](https://arc.net/l/quote/wyhijyca), etc.
- Control aims to be this catch-all.

We will refer to this overall plan, in which each controlled generation of AI aligns and builds the next until superintelligence is reached, as bootstrapping. Control's role is to keep that plan on the rails while it runs, not to carry out the alignment research itself.

## Modeling control

As previously mentioned in module 1, we can model control as

$$
P(\text{unacceptable}) = P(\text{unacceptable} \mid \text{scheming}) \times P(\text{scheming}) + P(\text{unacceptable} \mid \neg\text{scheming}) \times P(\neg\text{scheming})
$$

Minimizing $P(\text{unacceptable outcome} \mid \text{scheming})$ is the control agenda. By unacceptable outcome we refer to something which is catastrophic and irreversible.

Therefore, control mainly caters to threat models involving

- Sub-superintelligent AIs
- Schemers

It is up for debate as to how strong or catastrophically impactful a TUAI is, and how likely it is to be scheming. It could be the case that these AIs are not strong enough to impose catastrophic threats, which would mean "controlling" them should be less of a priority. After all, most of the legitimate existential risk comes from misaligned superintelligence, not misaligned transformatively useful intelligence.

## Reasons to upweight control anyway

There are also separate reasons why one may favor upweighting control work that do not fall under these criticisms

- Early AI scheming includes a variety of [threat models](https://www.lesswrong.com/posts/kcKrE9mzEHrdqtDpE/the-case-for-ensuring-that-powerful-ais-are-controlled) such as exfiltrations, rogue deployments, and research sabotage. As previously mentioned these all contribute to potential catastrophes. Preventing these from happening through control work is good across nearly all worlds. Thereby control proponents claim that control does not claim to build ASI safely; rather, by preventing early scheming catastrophes it makes the work environment more stable.
- Control also aims to flag early egregious misalignments to initiate potential policy or lab action — for example, by [catching AIs red-handed](https://www.lesswrong.com/posts/i2nmBfCXnadeGmhzW/catching-ais-red-handed).
- In the world where alignment is not solved and the AIs are misaligned, control is close to the only thing that still does anything. What it buys there is a floor rather than a solution — no escape, no unmonitored self-research, misalignment caught — holding a line and buying time and evidence even when every other agenda has failed.

## Weighing control against alternatives

Control is additive. It does not interfere with security, alignment, or verification interventions in a general sense. Therefore, we explore how counterfactually valuable allocating resources to control is in various scenarios.

<Demo id="additive-control" />

First, additive does not necessitate high-value. Control contributes to remedies for scheming and compiling misalignment evidence, but contributes almost nothing to reducing non-schemer induced slop. So its share of resources should track how much of the risk one expects to come from scheming rather than slop. Second, because the operative world is uncertain — the cruxes below are unresolved — a more robust move is to spread effort across additive fields (an [80/20](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pareto_principle#:~:text=80%25%20of%20consequences%20come%20from%2020%25) across several rather than all-in on one), sizing each by the kind of risk it actually addresses.

The main additive alternatives each have a place, and control tends to further them rather than compete with them. Verification and evaluation science works towards making alignment research independently checkable, and control provides the contained setting to develop and stress-test verification methods on untrusted models without the model subverting the work. [Scalable oversight](https://arc.net/l/quote/azhovenm), through debate, recursive reward modelling, and cross-checking outputs, aims to reduce the production rate of slop, and its protocols run on the scaffolding that control's monitoring provides, which also keeps a scheming model from colluding to defeat them.

Pause, coordination, and policy buy time, the lever the differential-acceleration harm said control needs, and control generates the red-handed misalignment evidence that makes a pause politically actionable. Alignment research proper, if it can make early AI trustworthy, removes the need to control it by acting on $P(\text{scheming})$ itself, though even here trust is earned under control, since control evaluations are how one accumulates evidence that a model is trustworthy or catches that it isn't before relying on it. Finally, capability restraint, declining to run RSI in this regime at all, is strengthened by the same red-handed evidence, which sharpens the case for declining or halting the loop.

So control is not merely additive but often enabling. It acts on the scheming case and on gathering misalignment evidence, and that evidence and containment is what lets the slop-focused alternatives run at all.
