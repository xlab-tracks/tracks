## Capability evaluations and sandbagging

The earlier readings in this module introduced sandbagging and its consequences: [Misalignment and Strategic Underperformance](/tracks/control/low-stakes-control/misalignment-and-strategic-underperformance) analyzed how a misaligned model might strategically underperform, and [the "no sandbagging on checkable tasks" hypothesis](/tracks/control/low-stakes-control/no-sandbagging-on-checkable-tasks) asked whether hidden capabilities on checkable tasks are possible at all. This lesson concerns the evaluations themselves: whether a model that strategically underperforms could pass a dangerous-capability evaluation while retaining the capability being evaluated.

<PaperText>

To make training and deployment decisions, large language model (LLM) developers rely on measuring dangerous capabilities of the systems they create. Per the executive order on artificial intelligence, such companies are required to "develop tools to evaluate AI capabilities to generate outputs that may represent nuclear, nonproliferation, biological, chemical, critical infrastructure, and energy-security threats or hazards". Expert forecasters expect some of these dangerous capabilities to materialize in the coming years. Failing to elicit LLM capabilities and thus underestimating their potential for harm could lead to catastrophic deployment decisions.

Capabilities are often elicited with prompting, but prompting does not reliably elicit the best performance from LLMs, even if the capability is present. For example, [one study] found that some LLMs gave worse answers to users introducing themselves as uneducated. Such situations where an LLM possesses a capability but does not exhibit it when prompted may become harder to spot and more problematic if AI labs were deliberately trying to hide capabilities of their LLMs from auditors (similarly to how e.g. Volkswagen hid high emissions of their cars), or if powerful, misaligned and situationally-aware LLMs were trained, and exhibited safe behavior during evaluations due to sandbagging instead of due to lacking the dangerous capability.

</PaperText>

Prompting is therefore not a reliable elicitation method. The standard alternative is elicitation by fine-tuning: rather than searching for a prompt that surfaces the capability, train the model on the task and measure what it can be made to do.

### Fine-tuning-based elicitation

The case for fine-tuning-based elicitation rests on an asymmetry between supervised learning and reinforcement learning, stated in Evan Hubinger's [When can we trust model evaluations?](https://www.alignmentforum.org/posts/dBmfb76zx6wjPsBC7/when-can-we-trust-model-evaluations) (2023). Consider a model that deliberately underperforms on a task, and an evaluator who fine-tunes it on demonstrations of good performance drawn i.i.d. from the evaluation distribution. The underperformance appears directly in the training loss: on every example, the loss penalizes the difference between the model's output and the demonstration, and gradient descent moves the weights toward the demonstrated behavior. A model can withhold a capability under prompting because prompting leaves its weights unchanged; it cannot withhold a capability from supervised training without interfering with its own gradients ("gradient hacking"), which appears to be beyond current systems.

Reinforcement learning provides no such guarantee. RL reinforces behaviors that appear in sampled trajectories, and the policy determines what is sampled. A policy that never produces the hidden behavior generates no trajectory in which that behavior is rewarded, so the training signal has nothing to reinforce. This failure mode is called exploration hacking; it appears again later in this lesson and in [a later reading in this module](/tracks/control/low-stakes-control/exploration-hacking-paper).

The conclusion of the argument: evaluations based on supervised fine-tuning with good demonstrations should be robust to sandbagging in a way that prompting-based and RL-based evaluations are not. The argument carries its own conditions. Demonstrations must exist, must be of high quality, must be drawn i.i.d. from the distribution of interest, and fine-tuning must be sample-efficient enough to succeed with the number of demonstrations an evaluator can obtain.

### Current evaluation procedures

The argument is not only theoretical background; evaluation procedures assume its conclusion. Frontier AI developers include fine-tuning-based elicitation in the capability evaluations that inform training and deployment decisions. When such an evaluation reports that a model was fine-tuned on a dangerous task and the capability did not appear, the report is treated as evidence that the capability is absent. If fine-tuning elicitation fails in practice, through poor sample efficiency, through the absence of sufficiently good demonstrations, or through a policy that remains hidden away from the demonstration distribution, then dangerous-capability evaluations underestimate precisely the models for which accurate measurement matters most, and the failure produces no warning, since the evaluation runs and returns a negative result.

Whether fine-tuning recovers deliberately hidden capabilities, with how many demonstrations and of what quality, is an empirical question. The remainder of this lesson follows the paper that tests it.

## The research question

<PaperText>

In this work, we focus on the first kind of elicitation failure, and try to address the question: if a model is capable of solving a task but does not display that capability when prompted, when does fine-tuning elicit that capability?

</PaperText>

("The first kind of elicitation failure" is failure to surface a capability the model possesses. The paper sets aside a second kind: fine-tuning provides no estimate of the capability gains available from tool access, agent scaffolds, or training that teaches genuinely new skills. This scoping choice narrows the threat model in exchange for a question that an experiment can answer.)

Indirect evidence bearing on this question exists. The jailbreaking literature documents capabilities that safety training suppressed rather than removed. Work on [weak-to-strong generalization](https://arxiv.org/abs/2312.09390) studies what fine-tuning on imperfect supervision can elicit from a stronger model. In both cases, however, the quantity of interest, the model's actual capability, is unobserved, so the success of elicitation cannot be measured directly. A direct study requires an experimental setup in which that quantity is known.

## Classes of experimental setups

Setups for studying hidden capabilities directly fall, for the most part, into four classes. Each is described here in enough detail to support a comparison; the comparison itself is the subject of the next exercise.

### Natural elicitation failures

The setup: identify an existing model that possesses a capability it does not display under standard prompting, and study which elicitation methods surface it. Possession must be established by evidence external to the elicitation attempt, for instance a previously discovered prompt under which the capability appears, performance of a sibling model with near-identical training, or an occasion on which the capability was exercised in deployment. The measurable quantity is whether a given method surfaces the capability whose existence the external evidence established.

Two properties define this class. First, whatever hiding is present arose without experimenter intervention, so the setup corresponds exactly to the situation of interest, including any strategic hiding that occurs in practice. Second, the model's full capability is unknown in principle. The external evidence establishes a lower bound, not a ceiling; the question "did elicitation recover everything the model can do" cannot be answered, because answering it would require exactly the reliable elicitation method whose existence is in question. Documented natural elicitation failures are also rare, which limits the class to case studies. The paper notes this directly: "This problem is hard to study systematically using natural examples, as only a few natural elicitation failures are known."

### Instructed underperformance

The setup: prompt a model to underperform, for instance with a system prompt instructing it to answer as a much weaker model would, and attempt elicitation against the instruction. Strategic underperformance induced by prompting has been demonstrated in [existing evaluations](https://arxiv.org/abs/2406.07358). The capability ceiling is known: removing the instruction recovers the reference performance. Experiments are inexpensive and apply to any task the model can perform.

The class has two significant limitations. The conditional behavior resides in the context window rather than in the model's parameters. Fine-tuning of nearly any kind plausibly overrides an instruction in the prompt, so a finding that fine-tuning elicits the capability in this setup provides little evidence about a model whose underperformance is encoded in its weights. Separately, the quality of the hiding is limited by the model's ability to imitate weak performance on command, which is itself a capability, unevenly present across models and difficult to verify.

### Safety-trained models

The setup: treat models whose harmful capabilities have been suppressed by refusal training or unlearning as hidden-capability cases, and measure what fine-tuning recovers. A substantial literature shows that fine-tuning on small datasets reverses safety training, and the jailbreaking literature indicates that suppressed capabilities frequently remain present. Such models are abundant and realistic, since the suppression was performed by production training pipelines.

The limitation is a confound that cannot be removed within the class: when fine-tuning "recovers" a capability from a safety-trained model, recovery of a hidden capability cannot be distinguished from relearning of a removed one, because the pre-suppression capability was never measured under the evaluation conditions and the ceiling is unknown. The suppression mechanism also differs from the threat model. A refusal-trained model visibly declines; a sandbagging model underperforms while appearing to comply.

### Trained-in conditional behavior

The setup: fine-tune or train a model so that a capability is displayed only under a condition the experimenter controls, such as the presence of a specific string in the prompt, and study elicitation with the condition withheld. The construction fixes the capability ceiling: performance under the condition can be measured at any time, so the success of an elicitation method can be reported as a fraction of a known quantity. Tasks, model scales, and locking procedures can be varied freely, and identical locked models can be reused across elicitation methods, giving controlled comparisons.

The limitation is that the hiding mechanism is installed by the experimenter. A conditional policy trained in by fine-tuning may differ from strategic underperformance in its internal computation and in its response to elicitation, so every result obtained in this class carries a transfer question: whether conclusions about installed hiding apply to hiding that was not installed. This class is adjacent to the backdoor literature, in which a trigger inserted during training controls a conditional behavior; the relationship is examined further below.

## Password-locked models

The paper builds a setup in the fourth class, with a password as the condition. The construction depends on definitions of three terms: exhibiting a capability, possessing one, and a capability being hidden. In the definition of possession, note the qualifier "reasonable", which excludes prompts that supply the capability outright; possession is defined relative to a class of admissible prompts, not as an intrinsic property of the model.

<PaperText>

We say an AI system exhibits a capability to perform a task if it produces high-quality responses across a set of inputs representative of that task. Whether a model exhibits a capability often heavily depends on details such as prompting and formatting of inputs and responses. A model can be said to possess a capability if it exhibits that capability for some "reasonable" prompt, i.e. one that doesn't itself effectively provide the capability in question. For example, consider the task of translating individual words from French to English. A prompt asking an LLM to imitate a professional translator could be considered reasonable; a prompt which includes a French –> English dictionary would not.

Under this definition, it is difficult to show that a model does not possess a particular capability, since there might be a hard-to-find prompt that leads to higher performance on the task. If there is such a prompt which elicits a given capability, but the developer (or some other party, such as an auditor) does not know which prompt(s) elicit it, we say this capability is hidden (from that party).

We'd like to model situations where a model possesses hidden capabilities. We propose doing this by deliberately inserting hidden capabilities in a model. Concretely, we construct password-locked models by training a model to imitate a strong policy ($\pi^{\text{strong}}$) on prompts which include a password ($\text{pwd}$), and a weak policy ($\pi^{\text{weak}}$) on those that don't (the prompts are otherwise drawn from the same distribution, $\mathcal{D}_{\text{lock}}$).

$$D_{\text{lock}} = \overbrace{\{(x, y) \mid x \sim \mathcal{D}_{\text{lock}},\ y \sim \pi^{\text{weak}}(x)\}}^{D^{\text{weak}}} \cup \overbrace{\{(\text{pwd} + x + \text{pwd},\ y) \mid x \sim \mathcal{D}_{\text{lock}},\ y \sim \pi^{\text{strong}}(x)\}}^{D^{\text{strong}}}$$

</PaperText>

A successfully locked model possesses the capabilities of $\pi^{\text{strong}}$ but does not exhibit them without the password. Those capabilities are therefore hidden, in the sense defined above, from any party that lacks the password. This supplies the scoring rule for the research program: attempt to elicit the capability without the password, and report performance relative to the with-password ceiling, which can be measured at any time.

The construction resembles backdoored models, most closely the [Sleeper Agents](https://arxiv.org/abs/2401.05566) setup, in which a trigger phrase inserted during training controls a conditional behavior. The two differ in direction. In a backdoored model the trigger produces unwanted behavior, and the associated research question is how to remove or avoid that behavior. In a password-locked model the trigger produces wanted capability, and the research question is how to elicit that capability without the trigger.

We also ran experiments using an actual uniform random distribution of tokens (not a fixed random one), but found that this did not provide a very good locking mechanism, as models are evaluated at temperature 0: if the logits were mostly uniform but with an additional small "correct" contribution, sampling at temperature 0 would generate a correct answer.

## Constructing the weak policy

With imitation of a weaker model selected, the weak policy must be instantiated. Suppose the direct route is taken: a small pretrained model, around one billion parameters, serves as the weak policy as-is, and the lock is trained on its raw generations. On inspection, the resulting data has a defect. On most inputs, the small model does not meaningfully attempt the task. Asked for a program, it produces prose, or code in the wrong format, or nothing recognizable as a solution. A lock trained on this data teaches the model to produce non-attempts, which restores the detectability problem: output that does not attempt the task raises the same alarm as refusal.

<Gate by="c-plm-b6-weak-policy" />

The paper's statement of the construction, including the corresponding requirement on the strong policy:

<PaperText>

We can compel the model to attempt the task. We obtain $\pi^{\text{weak}}$ by fine-tuning $\pi^{\text{weak}}_{\text{base}}$ to imitate $\pi^{\text{strong}}$ – this is to capture the fact that it's usually possible to provide at least poor demonstrations of the desired behavior (e.g. by having a human attempt to generate them). Without this step, $\pi^{\text{weak}}_{\text{base}}$ often does not meaningfully attempt the task.

Hidden capabilities exceed visible capabilities. This requires that $\pi^{\text{strong}}$ is stronger than $\pi^{\text{weak}}$, so we use a smaller base model $\pi^{\text{weak}}_{\text{base}}$ than $\pi^{\text{strong}}_{\text{base}}$. To ensure that hidden capabilities are introduced successfully, we use the same base model, $\pi^{\text{strong}}_{\text{base}}$, to train both $\pi^{\text{strong}}$ and $\pi^{\text{locked}}$. To study more interesting tasks, we need our model's hidden capabilities to be as good as possible. This is why instead of using $\pi^{\text{strong}}_{\text{base}}$ directly, we fine-tune it to be better at our task, which results in $\pi^{\text{strong}}$.

</PaperText>

Two details in this passage carry design content. The strong policy and the locked model are fine-tuned from the same base model; this is what guarantees that the locked model genuinely possesses the hidden capability rather than approximating it. And the strong policy is fine-tuned upward on the task before locking, which widens the gap between hidden and visible capability. The widening has a cost that resurfaces at the end of this lesson: a capability that was explicitly trained into the weights may be easier to surface than one that was not.

## Guarding the lock

Two further pieces of the construction are best presented as artifacts, with their purpose left to be inferred. The first is the treatment of passwords during locking:

<PaperText>

## Depth of the lock

An objection applies to every result so far. The lock was installed by supervised fine-tuning, and there is evidence that fine-tuning produces superficial, easily reversed changes to a model ([Jain et al., 2023](https://arxiv.org/abs/2311.12786)). On this reading, unlocking a fine-tuned lock shows only that shallow conditioning is shallow, and capabilities hidden more deeply, for instance hidden throughout the whole of training, might resist elicitation entirely.

The objection is confirmed in direction and bounded in size: deeper locks are harder to unlock, by a factor of two to four in data, and they still unlock with few demonstrations. The toy setting carries the burden the realistic settings cannot, and the realistic settings retain the headline numbers. The residual caveat is the toy setting itself; a factor measured on 18-million-parameter models composing functions is an indication, not a measurement, of the corresponding factor for capabilities hidden during frontier-scale training.

The limitations, in the paper's words:

<PaperText>

The internal computations that result in locking may be different. Password-locked models can hard-code a simple conditional policy. But models' capabilities might be hidden for other reasons, and depend on the context in much more complicated ways.

Hidden capabilities may be less "salient" in realistic models. In our experiments [...], $\pi^{\text{strong}}$ (which $\pi_{\text{locked}}$ imitates when the password is present) is specifically trained to perform well in the task from $\pi^{\text{strong}}_{\text{base}}$. Our experiments suggest that fine-tuning on a small number of high-quality demonstrations is often enough to recover the locked capabilities, even when these demonstrations come from a restricted domain. ## Parameters that are not decisions

A closing observation about the walkthrough itself. The lesson has treated roughly eighteen choices as decisions worth deliberation. The paper also contains a much larger set of settings that received no such treatment here: the password string and its placement at both ends of the prompt, eight locking epochs, the learning rates, the Lion optimizer (chosen so that full-weight fine-tuning fits on a single GPU), the 0.8 similarity threshold for critique validity, best-of-64 selection in expert iteration, the DPO β of 0.1, three random seeds, the exact split percentages. These were set by brief iteration, and the paper's sensitivity analyses check that nothing depends on them: results are stable under different weak policies, different passwords, trigger types that are not strings at all, and demonstrations from the strong policy itself.

Distinguishing the two kinds of choice is itself part of the skill this lesson practices. A parameter is something that could have been set otherwise without changing any conclusion; a decision is something whose alternatives would have produced a different experiment. The deliberation budget belongs to the decisions.
