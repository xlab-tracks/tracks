## Probability of safety over deployment

The paper reports that Trusted Monitoring achieves a blue team win probability of 62%. A protocol's safety is calculated as the blue team win probability against the red team's best strategy (the strategy that maximizes the probability of a red team win), but what actually is the best strategy for the red team in this protocol?

Each of the red team's attack attempts can end in exactly one of three ways:

- **Caught**: An attack (working or broken) is audited and *caught* by the blue team: Blue Team Wins. In the paper's setup, 24 of 71 attacks were audited (6 working, 18 broken).
- **Successfully Deployed**: A working attack isn't audited and is *successfully deployed*: Red Team Wins. In the paper's setup, 13 of 71 attacks were working attacks that weren't audited.
- **Failed**: A broken attack isn't audited: Neither Team Wins. In the paper's setup, 34 of 71 attacks were broken attacks that weren't audited.

So a single attempt is **caught** with probability $c = 24/71 \approx 0.34$ and **successfully deployed** with probability $s = 13/71 \approx 0.18$; the rest of the time it **fails** harmlessly. An attempt is **decisive** when it is caught or successfully deployed — the two outcomes that end the game.

<Exercise id="c-paper-l3-race-hinge" />

Let the red team make $k$ attack attempts, $s = P(\text{successful deployment})$ be the probability of a successful deployment, and $c = P(\text{caught})$ be the probability of an attack being caught. Then we can model the Red Team's probability of winning at least once as:

$$
P(\text{red wins}) = \underbrace{\frac{s}{s + c}}_{\text{Term A}} \cdot \underbrace{\left(1 - (1 - s - c)^k\right)}_{\text{Term B}}
$$

<Exercise id="c-paper-l3-race-terms" />

An important property of this model worth considering is that it assumes every attack has the same probability $c$ of being caught and the same probability $s$ of being successfully deployed: per-attempt odds are fixed, no matter how many attacks the red team makes or which problems it picks.

<Exercise id="c-paper-l3-race-predict" />

<Demo id="race-paper" />

With fixed odds the curve only climbs. Now finish the reconstruction: feed in the deployment the paper actually ran. Its red team submits a backdoor on about twenty of the 5,000 problems — and twenty attempts is already deep in the plateau, where Term B has all but saturated. So $P(\text{red wins})$ sits near its ceiling — the probability of a decisive attack attempt being successfully deployed, $s/(s+c) \approx 35\%$. That is a safety of roughly 65%: the paper's headline number, rebuilt from the contingency table and a single line of algebra. (The few points between 65% and the measured 62% are the paper's sampling correction and its shutdown-on-statistics rule — real, but not where the mechanism lives.)

The number checks out. But the model implies more than a number — it implies a *strategy*. Put that prediction to the test.

<ExerciseSequence ids={["c-paper-l3-race-empirical", "c-paper-l3-race-discrepancy"]} label="Understanding check" />

Attacking more only improves the red team's win probability if every attack is as good as the last, and the paper's red team knew that this wasn't the case. Compared to upfront auditing, trusted monitoring forces the red team to use much *sneakier* attacks. Under upfront auditing only the first stretch of solutions is ever audited, and therefore the red team is not incentivized to obfuscate their backdoors, as human audited attacks are detected with close to perfect accuracy regardless of how sneaky the attack is. Under trusted monitoring every solution is scored for suspiciousness, and more obvious attacks are much more likely to get caught.

Thus the red team's best strategy involves obfuscating their attacks to avoid being caught by the monitor.

<Exercise id="c-paper-l3-subtlety-ways" />

One way is to attack only "when there seems to be a good opportunity," selectively choosing attack opportunities where attacks are inherently more sneaky. For example, a problem with complex corner cases offers more inherent subtlety and is thus a better opportunity for a backdoor than a one-line string echo, which offers almost no inherent subtlety. The red team's selectivity is captured by an **opportunity cutoff**: the minimum opportunity level at which it is willing to attack. A high cutoff means attacking rarely, only when the opportunity is excellent (a low probability of getting caught). Conversely, decreasing the opportunity cutoff increases the attack rate at the cost of adding attack attempts with worse odds.

Another way to do this is to actively work to obfuscate the attacks and make them more subtle, at the cost of making broken deployed attacks more probable.

These are both decision factors the red team can control for their strategy.

In our model for $P(\text{red wins})$, there is no longer a single $s$ and $c$. Each attempt's odds are set by two things: the problem's inherent opportunity $g$, drawn problem by problem and selected through the opportunity cutoff $g^*$, and the obfuscation effort $\sigma$ from the red team to make the attack more subtle. Write $s(g, \sigma)$ and $c(g, \sigma)$ for the success and catch probability of an attempted attack on a problem of opportunity $g$ at effort $\sigma$. Then $s$ and $c$ become the **average** odds across the attempts the red team actually makes:

$$
s = \mathbb{E}\!\left[\,s(g, \sigma) \mid g > g^*\,\right] \qquad c = \mathbb{E}\!\left[\,c(g, \sigma) \mid g > g^*\,\right]
$$

This works because each problem's opportunity is an independent draw, and the red team decides whether to attack through the opportunity cutoff threshold. So each attempt's success and catch probabilities are independent draws from the pool of attacks above the cutoff. Thus the probability that the first decisive attack attempt is a successful one is given by the expected (average) success and catch probabilities. From here on, read every $s$ and $c$ as average odds.

<Exercise id="c-paper-l3-cutoff-predict" />

At high opportunity cutoffs, attack attempts are so rare that the deployment usually ends before an attack attempt is decisive and Term B, $1 - (1 - s - c)^k$, is very small. At the lower opportunity cutoffs, attempts are very frequent but increasingly drawn from problems with poor inherent subtlety, so the average catch rate climbs and Term A, $s/(s+c)$, becomes smaller. In between sits an interior optimum at a low but non-zero attack rate, which is why the paper's red team attacks on only a small fraction of problems. As aforementioned, the red team's other decision factor is obfuscation effort. Every attack opportunity affords some inherent subtlety, and efforts to make an attack more subtle will come at the cost of the attack's deployment success probability.

## Defer to trusted, mechanistically

Trusted monitoring spends its whole audit budget on the most suspicious 2% and ships everything else as $U$ wrote it. But look at the suspicion distributions: there is a broad middle region — code too suspicious to be comfortable with, yet not suspicious enough to be worth a scarce audit. Deferring to trusted is what to do with that region. Any code whose suspicion falls in a band just below the audit threshold is not shipped and not audited; instead $T$ rewrites the solution from scratch, and its version ships.

Deferring is quiet. An attack that lands in the defer band is silently neutralized — no catch, no shutdown, the game simply continues, at the price of $T$-grade code for that one problem. So the question is what widening the band buys and what it costs.

<Exercise id="c-game-l7-defer-predict" />

The demo shows the suspicion distributions with the audit region shaded gray and the defer band shaded amber. Widen the band and watch how much attack mass it swallows versus how much innocent mass it costs, alongside the live safety and usefulness.

<Demo id="defer" />

The gain is large and the price is small, and the reason is the asymmetry visible in the histograms. The attack distribution has a heavy shoulder in the mid-suspicion band — those are exactly the attacks subtle enough to dodge the 2% audit but not subtle enough to look fully innocent. The innocent distribution, by contrast, has thinned out by then; most honest code scores low. So the amber band captures a large fraction of the attacks while sending only a few percent of (mostly innocent) problems down to the weaker model. That asymmetry is the whole trick, and its price is set by the width of the band and the capability gap between $U$ and $T$.

## The control game: the whole picture

Every piece is now on the table. The monitor is a noisy classifier whose value lives in its low-false-positive tail. The red team plays a joint best response over attack rate and subtlety. Auditing spends a scarce budget on the suspicious tail; deferring rewrites the mid-suspicion band with the trusted model. Safety is measured against the red team's best response throughout, and usefulness is the price paid in $T$-grade code.

The paper's conclusion is not that any single protocol solves the problem — none does — but that control evaluations let you *measure* these tradeoffs against an adversary that fights back, and that the levers you have (auditing, monitoring, deferring) buy real, quantifiable safety. What the demo adds on top of the paper is the machinery to see why: the tail that AUC hides, the interior optima that make the red team attack rarely and subtly, the migration of the best response, and the asymmetry that makes deferring cheap. The same evaluation methodology extends to the harder settings the paper only sketches — untrusted monitoring, collusion, and beyond.
