**Hypotheses within reach of the initial experiment.** The first is that *supervised fine-tuning on good demonstrations recovers deliberately hidden capabilities*. This is the constructed-setting version of the elicitation argument; the experiment bears on it only for installed hiding, at small scale, and that conditionality is permanent. The second is that *RL-based elicitation fails when the visible policy never samples capable behavior*. This is the exploration-hacking mechanism in directly observable form: if the no-password policy is weak enough, the reward signal has nothing to reinforce.

**Confounds that could produce the same observations.** Lock quality confounded with elicitation strength: every elicitation result is a joint measurement of the method and the lock, so conclusions require validating the lock separately and reporting recovery relative to the with-password ceiling. A task too easy for the lock to bind: if the weak behavior occasionally produces correct answers, RL has signal, the hidden capability leaks, and the lock does not meaningfully constrain anything. Contamination between locking and elicitation data: if elicitation fine-tunes on problems seen during locking, recovery may be memorization. A trigger broader than intended: if the model learned to condition on unusual text rather than on the specific password, elicitation experiments measure prompt sensitivity rather than hidden capabilities. And scale and task specificity: results from small models on arithmetic may not transfer, which only repetition at scale can address.

**The results of the small experiment.** On the addition task, reinforcement learning does not unlock the model: across all four model sizes, accuracy without the password barely increases over training, and in no run does RL recover the locked capability. On the review task, the construction fails as an instrument: models collapse to generic five-star completions ("excellent product") even when the starting point is a model trained only on low-rated reviews, which shows the task is easy enough for a model to explore its way into good outputs from nearly any prior. The first result is a preliminary observation on the research question itself: the locked model's no-password behavior almost never produces a correct completion on hard problems, so the reward signal has no successful samples to reinforce, and RL-based elicitation fails. A model that deliberately underperformed in the same way would defeat RL-based evaluations by the same mechanism. The second result is a realized instance of a confound listed above: a task too easy for the lock to bind measures nothing. Both inform the scaled design in the sections that follow, and one of the confounds nevertheless reappears.
