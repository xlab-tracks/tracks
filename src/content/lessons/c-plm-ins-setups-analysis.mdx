Three considerations recur in comparing the classes: whether the model's true capability ceiling is known, whether the hiding mechanism corresponds to the threat model (a policy committed to underperforming, encoded in the weights, rather than one instructed to underperform), and whether the setup supports many inexpensive, controlled experiments. Against these considerations, the classes separate as follows.

**Natural elicitation failures.** Two properties define this class. First, whatever hiding is present arose without experimenter intervention, so the setup corresponds exactly to the situation of interest, including any strategic hiding that occurs in practice. Second, the model's full capability is unknown in principle. The external evidence establishes a lower bound, not a ceiling; the question "did elicitation recover everything the model can do" cannot be answered, because answering it would require exactly the reliable elicitation method whose existence is in question. Documented natural elicitation failures are also rare, which limits the class to case studies. The paper notes this directly: "This problem is hard to study systematically using natural examples, as only a few natural elicitation failures are known."

**Instructed underperformance.** The class has two significant limitations. The conditional behavior resides in the context window rather than in the model's parameters. Fine-tuning of nearly any kind plausibly overrides an instruction in the prompt, so a finding that fine-tuning elicits the capability in this setup provides little evidence about a model whose underperformance is encoded in its weights. Separately, the quality of the hiding is limited by the model's ability to imitate weak performance on command, which is itself a capability, unevenly present across models and difficult to verify.

**Safety-trained models.** The limitation is a confound that cannot be removed within the class: when fine-tuning "recovers" a capability from a safety-trained model, recovery of a hidden capability cannot be distinguished from relearning of a removed one, because the pre-suppression capability was never measured under the evaluation conditions and the ceiling is unknown. The suppression mechanism also differs from the threat model. A refusal-trained model visibly declines; a sandbagging model underperforms while appearing to comply.

**Trained-in conditional behavior.** The limitation is that the hiding mechanism is installed by the experimenter. A conditional policy trained in by fine-tuning may differ from strategic underperformance in its internal computation and in its response to elicitation, so every result obtained in this class carries a transfer question: whether conclusions about installed hiding apply to hiding that was not installed. In exchange, the capability ceiling is measurable at any time, which no other class provides, and the quantity under study, the gap between possessed and displayed capability, is observable only where the ceiling is measurable.

The paper builds its setup in the fourth class, with a password as the condition. The section that follows constructs it. In the definition of possession it gives, note the qualifier "reasonable", which excludes prompts that supply the capability outright; possession is defined relative to a class of admissible prompts, not as an intrinsic property of the model.
